It's 3am and the 10am deadline is fast approaching.

Raz0rBl@de opens a new can of energy drink. His fifth since midnight. He has been working on this job for two weeks without significant progress. If he fails his client is going to be pretty unhappy and they're not the type of people you want to upset. Unless you are particularly fond of baseball bats. Ding. A new email. Someone has just pushed a bug fix to one of the open source projects the target is running. Sweet. A bug in the protocol stack. Exploiting that should give him the access he so desperately needs. An hour later he has a working exploit. Ten minutes later he is finally in. The client just needs a copy of the customer database. Probably going to provide someone the competitive advantage to crush this little startup. But he has only just owned the Linux load balancer. He will need to find a way in to the database server. Damn. No known vulnerabilities and he doesn't have time to find a zero day. Change of plan. Attack the traffic. Get credentials, query the db, get out of there. Raz0rBl@de uploads a packet sniffer and fires it up. His heart sinks. He feels himself turning pale. Close to panic. Encrypted. They've encrypted the traffic from the cloud-hosted app servers. Who actually bothers to encrypt database traffic? An hour is wasted trying to find another way. Then another hour. It's 8am. Hope is lost. Raz0rBl@de gets up from his desk and gets into bed. Staring up at the ceiling he waits for the knocks at the door.

Andrew walks to his desk and sits down.

He looks at the clock on his computer. It's 8:45. Earlier than usual. Checking his emails one stands out. An IDS alert. One of the network processes on one of the gateways has spawned a shell. Strange. He logs in to investigate. The bash history is full of commands. Looks like the attacker didn't even bother to hide their tracks. Looks like they were targeting the database servers. A banner grab. Hmmm, a packet sniffer. The port scan showed that they must have been getting desparate. No other IDS alerts though. Andrew checks the database logs for access attempts but doesn't find anything unusual. Looks like they didn't get further than the gateway. He looks at the security announcements of the project that was exploited on his server. Nothing. A quick look at the version control commits. Bingo! Nasty. Still, nothing a u32 iptables rule can't stop until a proper patch is relase. He logs into the second gateway, adds the work around then fails the virtual ip address over to the standby. Logging onto the switch he disables the ports for the compromised server. That'll give him time to do some forensics later. Traffic has failed over. Probably worth rotating all the database TLS certificates. 30 seconds later he has generated and pushed a new private CA for the databases. Within 5 minutes all the certs will have rotated and the change management system will have reloaded all the necessary services. It's 9:00. Andrew gets up to make his second coffee of the day.

For any organisation, whether startup or enterprise, that values customer data and wants to improve their infrastructure security. pki.io is a service that provides simplicity, automation and security for the management of certificate infrastructure used by SSL/TLS encryption. Unlike developing a solution in-house, pki.io is cost-effective and makes not using encryption simply not worth the risk.

> Features

Simple

  • Easy to use command line administration tool
  • No coding or obscure openssl commands required
  • Fire-and-forget server agent

Secure

  • Quickly revoke and regenerate CAs and certificates
  • Separate service encryption without hassle
  • Built using standard libraries and established crypto algorithms

Automated

  • Admin-driven or fully automated, ideal for cloud deployments
  • Designed to scale from tens to thousands of servers
  • Optimized architecture for speed
